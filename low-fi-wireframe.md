How do low-fi wireframes help speed up the design process? 

Low fidelity wireframes help designer speed up the design process because they let designers test and validate different ideas prior to investing in high fidelity. 
Designers are solely focused on layout, structure, information hierarchy and content positioning in low fidelity wireframing. 
The visual aspects are not of their concern during this phase. Wireframes can also be tested fairly quickly and any issues identified during the usability tests can be fixed far more rapidly in contrast to high fidelity versions. 
It is also much faster to get feedback on low fidelity wireframes from product managers and developers. Their feedback can be actioned much more faster on a low fidelity wireframe than on a high fidelity one. Wireframes allow design teams to validate ideas, experiment and fail without severe consequences as opposed to high fidelity versions.


How do UX teams use wireframes for early feedback and iteration? 

Design teams use wireframes prior to progressing onto high fidelity versions. The wireframes can be tested with users and also evaluated by the product manager and developers. 
The product manager and developers can point out issues in the wireframes if needed. The designer can then iterate based on these evaluations. 
User testing sessions can also be conducted where the designer can communicate findings to product manager and developers who can then provide more feedback to address the usability issues identified in user testing. 
This way the design team saves time before progressing onto high fidelity versions of the design.

What are some common mistakes designers make when creating wireframes? 

The most common mistakes that designers make when designing wireframes is that they are not entirely treated as tools of exploration, but rather as versions of an almost finished design. 
A designer should not be obsessed with a lot of visual detailing on a wireframe. A wireframe is a tool that allows designers to validate ideas and experiment.  
Sometimes  designers make wireframes in isolated screens instead of focusing on user journeys. It is always better to focus on specific flows rather than isolated wireframes. 
This is because wireframes on their own can look fine, but when you examine flows, it may become apparent that there are certain issues that occur as the user navigates through the flow. 
Sometimes, designers may find it difficult to explore alternatives to their wireframes because they spent time creating the wireframes. 
This can lead to them failing to properly explore different ideas and moving too quickly to high fidelity versions. This leads to missing out on better solutions. 
Another common issue is not getting proper feedback from the product manager and the developers on the wireframes created. Wireframes are not meant to be created in isolation. 
They are useful when they are shared with key stakeholders because you can gather useful feedback in this manner. Without the feedback, wireframes lose their value.
