How do PMs decide which UX improvements to prioritize? 

Product managers often have to consider several factors in order to determine how best they are able to achieve business and user goals.  They often evaluate the impact a product will have on the user’s behaviour and ultimately how this in turn will have an impact on business outcomes. 
Within the context of focus bear, a product manager might look at how the onboarding process completion rate has an impact on user retention and conversion.
A product manager will also need to determine just how much effort will be required by the design and development team if they are to make changes to the product. 
For example, usability test results might indicate that there is a significant drop off that occurs for users during the onboarding process for the focus bear app. 
A product manager will seek to make changes to the app to ensure that drop off’s remain minimal, with a specific aim to retain users. A product would determine how much work would need to be done by the UX and development team To successfully address the issues in order to minimise drop offs during onboarding, and retain users , so the product can be scaled better overtime.
A product manager would also look at how any issues that are currently affecting the product would hinder the product from meeting business goals and outcomes. Product managers have to determine where they are going to focus their limited resources. The UX team helps the product manager by providing evidence about a specific design issue can negatively impact user and business goals. 
In addition, the UX team will also provide the product manager with a suitable way to address the issue they identified in order to ensure that user experience is optimal and that business goals are met. 





What factors influence prioritization? (e.g., user impact, development effort, business goals) 

A product manager often has to ensure that a product is able to improve the overall user experience for users. Any changes that are made to the product will need to improve the user experience. 
This is a key area that a product manager will look at in order to determine if it needs to be prioritised further to improve the product. This is because this particular area has the potential to impact not just the user experience but also the business goals. 
Furthermore, a product manager will also look at how a product has the ability to influence business goals such as user retention, and user satisfaction. 
Therefore, any issues with the product will need to ensure that the business goals are addressed properly. On the contrary, a product manager needs to determine how much development work needs to be undertaken by the time and also prioritise resources accordingly. 
For example, a minor improvement to the product that could positively impact user retention will be prioritised over adding a new feature that has more complexity involved, and slower immediate returns. The product manager also will determine whether a particular issue with the product needs to resolved immediately or whether it can be addressed over other more issues that exist. 
For example,  if users are complaining about not being to read the text clearly in the digital product, the product manager will need to resolve this immediately because leaving it unaddressed will lead to significant customer drop offs from the product. This in turn will impact business revenue quite badly. 



How can UX designers make a strong case for usability improvements? 

The best way for a UX designer to make a strong case for making usability improvements to a product is by re-stating the improvements that could occur by addressing a usability issue with the product. 
The UX designer would need to showcase how resolving the usability will positively impact the user experience and business goals/outcomes.  
The best way to approach this is by providing evidence. This can include user testing session recordings, user interviews fingerings, survey findings, and other direct observations that can successfully demonstrate how users become confused, how they make mistakes or finding the interface complicated and if they are abandoning key tasks mid process. 
For example, if new users are finding the onboarding process for focus bear to be confusing, and long winded, then this could have an adverse impact on the user retention rate and reduce the numbers of users that successfully become active with focus bear. 
A strong case made by a UX designer would also need to state clearly how making usability improvements will impact business goals positively alongside user goals. 



What frameworks help balance UX work with feature development? (e.g., RICE, MoSCoW) 

Many frameworks such as RICE and MOSCOW can allow a product manager to determine what particular items in the product backlog needs to be given first priority. 
Time and budget constraints are quite common within the product development process. For example, it might have become obvious with user testing that there are significant issues with the “‘statistics” screen of the focus bear app. On the contrary, the product manager might be trying  to introduce a new feature into the app in order to scale the product and increase its user base. 
These aforementioned frameworks allow the product manager to compare what option to implement first and foremost in an organised, structured and rational manner. 
The RICE framework helps a product team determine how a particular feature will benefit the most number of users, how deep the impact will be of the Change that will be made to the product or introduction of the new feature to the product,  how certain the product team is with the level of evidence they have to implement a new feature or make changes to an existing feature, and the amount of work that will be required to make those particular changes. 
On the contrary, the MOSCOW framework helps a product manager determine what particular features are going to be needed and are vital for the current product release, in contrast to which ones can be deferred for later. 


If a PM tells you there’s no time for a UX improvement, how would you respond? 

When a product manager communicates to UX designer that there is no time for making a UX improvement, it is important for the designer to understand the root cause behind this issue. 
The root cause could be due to lack of engineering capacity, priorities that are shifting or competing against one another, lack of meaningful primary and secondary evidence for making the change that clearly demonstrates that it will provide meaningful value to the product or due to budget constraints. 
A UX designer must clearly explain to the product manager how not making the UX improvement would impact the product and the overall user experience. 
The UX designer must also demonstrate any relevant evidence they have in the form of Primary and secondary research that the improvement is needed due to various issues being experienced by users. 
When there is a significant amount of constraints that do prevent the UX improvement from taking place (‘due to budget constraints for example), the UX designer should then document the UX improvement that needs to take place and include this in the backlog. 
This allows the product team to implement the UX improvement in the future. A competent and mature UX designer understands that constraints are real and they learn to work in a manner that ensures that they can bring about the most value within those constraints.


How can UX designers ensure that important usability issues don’t get ignored? 

A UX designer needs to ensure that when any usability issues are identified via user testing and/or UX audits, that those identified issues are not simply ignored. They can do this by providing evidence regarding those aforementioned usability issues. 
They can also communicate in a manner that is clear and demonstrates to the product team the impact those issues have on user experience and on business metrics. It is not enough for a UX designer to simply say “there are major issues with the onboarding for focus bear, and the settings screen is also having issues”. 
The designer needs to provide evidence in the form of usability test reports and findings that clearly demonstrate how the issues occur. If an issue cannot be addressed immediately, the designer should seek to document the issue in depth and add this into the backlog. This allows the product team to address the problem at a later date. 

What’s the best way to present UX work as a business priority rather than a "nice-to-have"? 

The most tangible way to present UX work as a business priority is by actively connecting the usability issues a user might experience with business metrics. 
It is also important to discuss how those particular issues would Impact business goals in the short and long term.
A UX designer might communicate to the product team that the current “‘statistics screen for focus bear is having an adverse impact on overall user satisfaction and retention due to how confusing this screen is and how it is overwhelming users quite badly”. 
In order to get their point across, the UX designer needs to point out the user problem clearly to the product team. In addition, they need to provide suitable evidence via primary and secondary research about the problem and how it occurs. 
Then they must communicate business value about what can occur if left unaddressed versus how things change if they address those particular issues.
