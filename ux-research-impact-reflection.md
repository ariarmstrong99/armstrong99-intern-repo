How does UX research influence design iterations and feature development? 

UX research has a key impact on the entire design process. This is particularly the case when it comes to design iterations and feature development. 
UX research helps the product team make design decisions based on concrete evidence. The research process in ux is responsible for identifying the needs of users, their pain points, behaviours and goals. 
Gaining an understanding on these key factors allows UX designers to better understand what problems need to be focused on solving. The problem statement definition is fundamental here and without UX research this would not even be possible. 
UX research can be divided into secondary and primary.  When it comes to influencing feature development, UX research helps the product team decide what exactly should be built and what needs to be improved upon. Instead of focusing on developing features that are based entirely on assumptions, ux research provides evidence for the team to them decide which feature will have the biggest payoff in terms of value for the business and in improving the overall user experience for the users  if the team worked on the implementing the particular feature. 
For design iterations, UX research can help the product team identify where exactly the users struggle in order to accomplish a particular task they are performing within the product. User testing results and user interviews allow the team to find out the exact reasons behind this and then they can iterate based on the data. 



What are examples of research-driven design decisions? 

Research driven design decisions are based on concrete evidence. Design changes are then made based on the actual evidence. 
Within the context of focus bear mobile app, a usability test might indicate that several users have difficulty using the settings menu within the app. 
Based on such a finding, the product team can then proceed to make improvements to the settings screen. Similarly, when user testing results show that 20% of users are abandoning the onboarding process. 
The design team can proceed to conducting user interviews and also evaluate the analytics to determine why exactly this is occurring. Once they find the root cause, they can then proceed to make improvements to the onboarding process and redesign the onboarding. 


What happens when design choices ignore user feedback? 

When a product team decides to ignore user feedback and makes design choices without evaluating the analytics and UX research findings, it creates sub optimal user experiences. 
A sub optimal user experience then leads to poor business outcome and a product that struggles to retain users. 
It can also lead to the design team solving the wrong problem and create experiences that are simply incongruent with user needs. 
All of this can have a negative impact on the business outcomes. UX designers need to ensure that they evaluate UX research findings properly. 
They need to evaluate the user testing reports, conduct user interviews, primary and secondary forms of research and also ensure that they take into account product strategy, product product and business goals, budget constraints and other technical constraints. 

How can user research and usability testing prevent major UX failures? 

User research and user testing are key aspects of the UX process. Usability testing usually occurs right after the design phase of the double diamond process. 
UX research and usability tests help the product team identify issues that are not explicit. It allows the team to understand if they are solving the right problem, what are the specific user needs,and how this aligns with business goals. 
It is through UX user research that the team can determine if the problem they are solving is going to produce enough value for those users. Usability testing allows designers to identify where exactly users are struggling as they interact with the product interface and helps the team address the usability issues.



If research contradicts a designer’s personal preference, what should happen? 

When research is contradicting the designers personal preferences, the designer needs to exercise maturity and their expertise in the matter. 
They should always defer to the research findings since this is based on evidence. Within the context of the focus bear app mobile onboaring process, a designer may prefer to implement a minimalistic approach to the onboarding, with minimal user guidance. 
However, if the user testing results clearly indicate that the users were struggling to complete the onboarding process due to lack of guidance and abandoning the process altogether, then the designer needs to take this into account. 
The designer would need to evaluate the actual results, consider the business goals and also the user mental models in order to determine  the most appropriate solution. 
A UX designer must understand that UX research has the specific purpose of ensuring all decisions in regards to the designs are based on how users interact with the product and how this aligns with business needs.



How can UX designers ensure that their work aligns with real user needs? 

UX research allows designers to align their work with actual user needs. User interviews help a user understand things about the users, including their mental models. 
Usability testing helps designers evaluate where users are experiencing difficulty when they interact with a product’s user interface. 
User interviews helps them clarity why they experience those aforementioned issues. In addition, UX designers can also review product analytics and analyse feedback to make sure they are solving the right problem. 
Alignment with user needs is ongoing. If at all the UX research indicates that the design direction taken is incorrect and fails to align with user needs and business goals, this needs to be rectified by the UX designers to risk creating a sub-optimal user experience and product. 


What risks come with relying on assumptions rather than research when designing an 
interface? 

When a UX designer tends to rely more on their assumptions instead of the research, they may end up solving the wrong problem altogether. Many designers tend to have a powerful intuition or “gut feeling”. 
But intuition cannot be relied upon in order to create experiences for users that meets their needs and aligns with business goals. A UX designer might think that a better feature is needed for an existing app. 
However, if user testing results indicate that the existing features are difficult for users to understand, then this area would need to be addressed first prior to introducing new features. 
When a designe relies more on assumptions, they risk creating experiences that confuse and frustrate the user. Furthermore, this can lead to poor business outcomes for the product over time.
