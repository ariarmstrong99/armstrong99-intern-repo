
Who are considered vulnerable populations, and what challenges might they face in digital spaces? 

A member of a vulnerable population can be comceptualised within the UX paradigm  as an individual that has faces additional barriers, challenges and risks when they interact with digital products. 
More specifically regarding focus bear as a product, a member of a vulnerable population is an individual that has adhd, autism, experienced mental health issues, or has a learning disability.  
These aforementioned individuals often face issues when it comes to maintain their attention, have slower informational processing capabilities, have issues with short term memory, struggle to comprehend complex instructions and can get more easily overwhelmed by a sub optimal user experience due to low frustration tolerance. 
An Individual with adhd might become more agitated and abandon the onboarding process for focus bear app altogether if the value proposition is not explicit early during the onboaring and if they perceive the onboarding process to be taking “too long”. 
Members of vulnerable populations can also include older people, individuals experiencing low digital literacy due to poverty  and other individuals that are experiencing severe hardship,  snd people with poor vision or other disability (deaf and/or mute for example) These types of users often deal with barriers that can make it more difficult to use a digital product to solve the problem they are trying to solve by using the product in the first place and derive a positive user experience from their interaction with the products interface.





What ethical considerations are important when designing or working with neurodivergent individuals? (Hint: Avoid overwhelming UX, respect sensory needs, ensure clear communication.) 

It is important to take into account the needs of neurodivergent individuals when designing for their needs or working with them as peers. 
It is vital to reduce the impact of cognitive overwhelm and cognitive load. All interfaces should aim to provide information in a clear manner that breaks down complex tasks into more manageable steps. 
It is also important to avoid unnecessary cluttering the interface or having too much information for the users to process and remember the information.  
It is also important to ensure that the sensory needs of neurodivergent people are taken into account. Flashing animations or lights can create a user experience that can overwhelm a neurodivergent user. 
Furthermore, it is also vital to have clear communication and instructions to help users understand feedback things better. 
Using language that is u clear, complex and ambiguous can be problematic because it increases the level of cognitive effort required in order to process this particular information. 
On the contrary, when working alongside neurodivergent peers, it is important to be patient since these individuals process information differentially and take more time. 
It is also important to provide them with clear expectations and give them more time to articulate themselves. 



How can you make interactions and content more accessible for people with ADHD or Autism? (e.g., simple language, predictable navigation, reducing cognitive load) 

Individuals with adhd and autism can experience issues with executive functioning, informational processing, have sensory overwhelm issues and struggle to navigate interfaces that are complex in nature. 
In order to make interactions and content more accessible, a UX designer can focus on minimising the amount of unnecessary effort to make the user experience far more clear and straightforward to navigate. 
For example, a simple and easy to understand onboarding process for the focus bear mobile app that communicates the value proposition with more ease, and that is not long, would lead to higher levels of completion amongst people with adhd and autism. 
It is also important to reduce the total cognitive load and this in turn can be achieved by breaking down tasks into smaller action steps, limiting the level of imformation that is shown at a time and by providing clear feedback once a user completes an action successfully. 
In a digital product such as focus bear, making things more accessible can translate to guiding users through the onboarding process step by step that helps them understand why they are being asked certain things. 
In this manner, users are able to better focus on things that are most important instead of being distracted by trying to figure things out themselves. 

How can we support neurodivergent team members in a professional setting? (Hint: Be clear in communication, respect different working styles, and allow flexibility.) 

Neurodivergent team members play a crucial role in a design team. It is important that team members communicate clearly about job expectations, and deadlines, in a manner that is non ambiguous. 
Also providing instructions in written format and helping document design decisions can help ensure that each team member can understand the document clearly is important in order to facilitate collaboration. 
It is also crucial to recognise that not every team member thinks or conducts themselves in the same manner. 
Some team members can prefer written information that is more detailed, whilst some others may provide their best work with more agency. 
Some team members may struggle more with written communications and may prefer small briefing’s in stand up meetings to help them process the information faster. 
It is important to be flexible to allow different team members to perform at a high Ievel. 
Treating neurodivergent team members in a patronising manner, minimising their potential, second guessing their ability and assuming they are lazy, can impair the collaborative environment within a design team with neurodivergent team members. 
Being inclusive also means treating neurodivergent members in a manner that respects their dignity, whilst also ensuring that they are not treated differently compared to other team members.

What are some common UX or communication pitfalls that might make Focus Bear less accessible or supportive? 


Focus bear’s main target audience is neurodivergent users. For this reason it’s important to ensure that all communication is supportive and inclusive. 
Providing users with an experience that requires them to strain themselves cognitively to process information and overwhelming them with too many decisions at a time can make focus bear less accessible. 
This is particularly the case for adhd users that have a low frustration tolerance and struggle in the presence of vague terminology, and instructions that are lengthy. 
Having navigation that is inherently inconsistent can also problematic in terms of accessility for neurodivergent users of focus bear, which in turn make the product less supportive and accessible to use. 
For example, if certain buttons and settings work differently across the focus bear app, then users are forced to constantly relearn how the interface actually works and this in turn can increase user frustration. 
In terms of communication, it is important to help users understand what certain terms mean within the context of the app. 
For example, a user might not necessarily know want a habit or routine means within the context of the focus bear app. 
They might have their own assumptions about what a habit or routine is if this is not explained Clearly in an intuitive manner in the product. 
Users can also become overwhelmed with frequent notifications from the focus bear app that destroy their ability to stay on task and focus. 
Whilst the notifications are meant to help the user, they can break a neurodivergent users focus and force them to switch context. 
This in turn depleted their mental bandwidth further and can cause frustration.
