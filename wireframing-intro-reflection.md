What is a wireframe, and how does it differ from a prototype? 

A wireframe is basically a skeleton version of a user interface. The main purpose of a wireframes is to help a designer focus on solving the main issues with usability, before proceeding to adding visual finesse. 
So essentially a wireframe helps a designer establish what exactly needs to go on a particular screen, where exactly it ought to be positioned and what specific actions a user will be able to take. 
The fundamental purpose of a wireframe is to focus on hierarchy, and structure. For example, when redesign the onboarding for focus bear, a wireframe might help establish whether the onboarding can be reduced to 2-3 steps, and what particular information is vital and what information can be removed. 
In contrast, a prototype tests how the design is meant to work when a user interacts with the interface. 
A wiframe is entirely static in nature, whereas a prototype is clickable, allows a user to navigate from one screen to another and complete tasks. 
Prototypes are made once a wireframe is confirmed. Prototypes are essentially a simulation and can help determine if users can achieve their goals by interacting with the product in a successful manner. 



What are the key differences between low-fidelity and high-fidelity wireframes? 


The primary difference between a low fidelity and high fidelity wireframe is in the level of depth. A low fidelity wireframe is meant to help users explore ideas rather quickly. 
It tends to focus more on structure, hierarchy, and positioning of content. 
A low fidelity wireframe is quite basic in nature since this is basically allowing the design team to validate and test design concepts prior to proceeding to a more polished version of the user interface. 
In contrast, a high fidelity wireframe tends to be a polished version of the low fidelity wireframe. 
This will include a more detailed version of things like colours, content that is realistic, and typography. 
The ultimate goal is to ensure that the user interface will look and feel a certain way prior to proceeding with the development process. 


When should wireframes be used in the UX design process? 


A wireframe is typically created after conducting and Synthesizing user research. The low fidelity wireframe is created prior to proceeding to a high fidelity version once the design team is satisfied with the information architecture, layout and content positioning on a low fidelity wireframe. 
For example , within the context of the focus bear onboarding, it might have been discovered that users are deciding to abandon the onboarding process due to how lengthy and confusing the process is according to user perception. 
A competent UX designer would first create wireframes in order to explore the myriad ways that the onboarding flow for focus bear can be organised for the redesigned version. 
This will allow the design team to see whether users are responding well to the redesign or still struggling. 
It is quite easy to simply change the layout, restructure a flow and come up with various alternatives prior to proceeding to high fidelity designs.


How do wireframes improve collaboration between UX, PMs, and developers? 

A wireframe improved the collaboration between UX designers, products managers and developers because it allows them to visualise the proposed solution prior to investing more time and energy into making it more fleshed out. 
Without having a wireframe as a reference point, the discussions can become quite abstract in nature. A UX designer can verbally articulate the design solution but this might be interpreted quite differently by the product manager and developer. 
A wireframe helps a product manager determine if the solution is actuallly going to address the main user problem that was identified earlier in the research phase. 
It also helps the product manager determine if the design solution is aligning with product goals, and whether or not the right problem is even being solved. When a developer looks at a wireframe, it helps them determine if there are any technical concerns that ought to be discussed prior to proceeding onto high fidelity. 
It also help them understand how users will move navigate through the experience. Within the context of the onboarding process for focus bear, a redesigned wireframe might be evaluated by a product manager. The product manager could then determine if every step is needed. 
The developer might mention whether the solution will have certain constraints and challenge during development.  In this manner, the design team can make adjustments to the wireframes before proceeding to high fidelity and development. 



What are the benefits of starting with wireframes rather than jumping into high-fidelity design? 

When a design team starts with a wireframe, they can find out if they  are actually solving the correct problem. By going directly into high fidelity, a design team might focus more on the visual aspects of the design such as typography, colours, and other visual details. 
However, vital imformation such as structure, information hierarchy and usability will be overlooked as a result. 
For example, if focus bear is aiming to redesign their onboarding, it becomes more important if users can determine if users actually understand the onboarding steps properly rather than focusing on the overall aesthetic design or the Color of the bears on the screen. 
Proceeding with wireframes allows for changes in a much easier manner. Wireframes also make the process of collaboration much smoother between product manager, developer and UX designer. 
The developer and product manager can evaluate the wireframe and determine if there will be any technical constraints or if the right problem is being solved, and whether  If there are any modifications to be made. 

How can wireframes help identify usability issues early? 

Wireframes can help designers detect any usability issues early on because they allow the product manager and developer to evaluate them prior to proceeding on with high fidelity designs.  
Simply by glancing at a wireframe, it becomes easy to spot any issues such a problems with imformation architecture, too many steps that can be avoided, user flows that are unclear and information that is inaccurate or incomplete. 
For example, for the focus bear onboarding processs, wireframes can be made and tested in order to allow designers to determine if the new onboarding process has too many steps or if the process is confusing. 
The issue can then be rectified much easier since the design is still quite malleable.  



If a stakeholder asks for a high-fidelity design too soon, how would you explain the value of wireframing? 

I would simply explain that wireframing is a crucial step of the UX process. I would emphasise that skipping this particular step could result in more time being lost if changes need to made to the high fidelity versions. I would focus on articulating how wireframes are a useful utility in helping designers explore ideas, gather important feedback, and make changes earlier on prior to proceeding to high fidelity. 
For example, for the redesigned focus bear onboarding process, if the stakeholders tell me directly to move onto high fidelity designs, I would simply mention that focusing on wireframes first would help us determine if the onboarding has any issues with users. 
If any issues are found, I would explain that we can rectify them much sooner in wireframe format, prior to proceeding with high fidelity.
