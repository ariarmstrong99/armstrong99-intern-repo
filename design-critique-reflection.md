What is design critique, and why is it a crucial part of the UX process? 

A design critique is an essential part of the UX process. 
This usually entails a discussion that is structured in nature whereby the UX design team will seek to evaluate a design and provide input on how it is addressing the problem statement (problem that was identified). 
A design critique session aims to simply determine if a solution the UX team is working towards can be used by the user in an efficient, clear and non confusing manner. 
In addition, the purpose of these sessions is also to ensure that the business goals and user goals are properly aligned. 
The UX team will seek to evaluate the design solutions in accordance to the UX principles, insights received from the user research and business objectives for the solution (product) that is being worked on by the team.  
Design is an iterative process and product managers and developers can also be part of the design critique sessions. 
In this manner, a UX designer can help convey to the product manager and developers what the usability issues are, and how the solution that they are currently working on addresses the user goals and business goals. This is vital to ensure that a product is successful.




What are the key principles of giving helpful, constructive feedback? 

For any feedback to be helpful in nature, it needs to be clear, honest, easy to understand, objective, specific and should focus exclusively on how the design can be improved upon. 
This would mean that feedback would explore what aspects of the design solution are positive, what aspects need to be altered/ improved, and why exactly those alterations are needed and how it will impact the user experience and product goals/business goals. 
Constructive feedback is always evidence based and should help the designers implement the feedback. 



How should designers receive and apply critique without taking it personally? 

It is normal for designers to be quite attached emotionally to their work outputs due to the amount of time,energy and effort that they invest into those aforementioned outputs.  
The fundamental objective of a design critique is not to find fault with the designer at a personal level or question their ability to produce relevant work outputs within their role. 
Instead, the objective of a design critique is to help further improve and refine the design solution. Therefore, UX designers should seek to ask questions during the design critique sessions in order to establish the underlying reasoning behind the critique of their work. 
Once feedback is provided, it needs to be properly vetted by going through the user research, business/product  goals and any other constraints. 
A competent and mature UX designer is often characterised by their ability to take feedback about their work in a manner that does not personalise the critique, or view the critique as a personal attack. 
Instead, they would listen to the feedback, ask for clarifications, ask for evidence and then work on making improvements.


What are common mistakes when giving or receiving design feedback? 

During a design critique session, it it’s important to be specific, clear, and  non ambiguous with the feedback. It is not appropriate to make vague statements based on “intuition” or ambiguous subjective criterion that is not evidence based or line with the visual design, product, design thinking and UX principles. 
A mistake designer often make when they receive feedback is to instinctively defend feedback they receive. This mainly occurs due to the attached they have towards the work they have produced. However, this hinders growth and is an obstacle to learning for the designer. 
On the contrary, being overly receptive to feedback without ensuring that it aligns with the user research, product goals, and other limitations is the other side of the spectrum. 
Any design critique or design feedback is best when it is specific, targeted, clear, non ambiguous, provides explicit reasoning, is evidence based and facilitates the ongoing process of making improvements to the overall product. 


What is the difference between personal opinion and objective UX critique? 

A personal opinion is not based on UX principles, design thinking principles, product thinking, visual design design principles, and UX research. Any objective UX critique is always based on evidence. 
Such as UX research, usabily principles, accessibility guidelines, design thinking principles and product thinking/product goals. 
Ultimately UX critique is aimed at improving how design can solve the problem that the team has identified and is working towards solving with design.



If someone gives vague feedback like "I don’t like this", how would you get more useful input? 

I would ask the person a series of questions to get more clarity behind why the statement “I don’t like this” was made. 
It is important for me as a UX designer to understand what aspects of the design I need to improve upon. I would seek clarification on what feels ineffective, are there specific areas of the design they do not like and ask them why. 
I would probe further until I have specific information as to “why” they do not like the design. I would seek to establish whether they do not like the design or parts of it because of any inherent usability problems or concerns or whether it fails to meet user and/ or product/business goals. 

How can you ensure that design critique sessions are productive rather than overwhelming? 


It is best to focus design critique sessions on specific issues. Otherwise this can lead to an overwhelming session that focuses on too many areas. This can make it significantly harder for designers to implement the feedback to improve the design solution. 
The extent to which feedback is provided should also be established clearly. 
For example, the UX team should focus on discussing a specific flow and how it is impacting user retention in its current state. Instead of going through multiple aspects of the product, the aforementioned approach is much cleaner. 
This allows for a more precise discussion, and allows the designer to implement the changes instead of becoming overwhelmed.
