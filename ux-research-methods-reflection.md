What are the two main types of UX research? (Qualitative vs. Quantitative) 

The two main types of research are quantitative and qualitative. Qualitative research helps a designer understand how users think, behave, what they are motivated by, and the challenges faced by users. 
Qualitative research tends to focus more on why exactly users behave in a certain manner. 
Common methods such as user interviews and usability testing are crucial in the UX process. 
On the contrary, quantitative research tends to focus more on numerical data and patterns that can be observed and measured.  
This type of research allows designers to understand what exactly users are doing and how often this tends to take place. 
Surveys and a/b tests are examples of quantitative research that are crucial to the UX process. 


What are common UX research methods (e.g., user interviews, usability testing, surveys, A/B testing)? 

UX research can be categorised into quantitative research and qualitative research. There are myriad UX research methods available for a designer that can help them during the UX process. 
User interviews allow UX designers to understand users in depth by speaking to them and trying to gain insight into their behaviours, mental models, pain points, motivations and needs. 
Usability testing entails conducting tests on a product’s  interface with users and then observing them interact with the aforementioned interface. 
The fundamental idea here is that by observing users interact with the product interface a designer is able to pin point where exactly the user is struggling, becoming confused or failing to understand how the interface works altogether. 
In contrast, surveys are often used to gather feedback from a large number of users. Surveys allow designers to get information on user satisfaction levels, and help identify trends across a base of users.
On the other hand, a/b testing helps designers compare two versions of a design in order to determine which one performs at a better level. 
A product team might test out two different versions of a settings screen for an app in order to determine which version leads to a higher level of completion for certain tasks. 


When should you use observational research vs. self-reported feedback? 

A competent UX designer often knows what specific research method to pick based on their unique context. 
Observational research methods such as usability testing and field studies allow a UX designer to identify problems by observing the behaviour of users directly. 
Observational research methods are used when a UX designer is trying to gain an understanding of what users are actually doing. Within the context of the focus bear mobile app, a user might indicate that the onboarding process was “normal”, but user testing sessions might indicate that the user hesitates and has to question why they are being asked certain questions during the onboarding. 
Observational research is quite useful because it allows UX designers to observe directly and then identify where exactly users are having issues, even if they indicate everything is “‘normal”. In contrast to observational research, self reported feedback is often used when a UX designer is trying to understand the opinions, motivations, feelings and perceptions of users. 
User interviews and surveys are good examples of self reported methods. These particular methods allow UX designers to understand how users perceive a product and why they tend to think a certain way. 
Within the context of the focus bear app mobile onboarding process, a user might abandon the onboarding process altogether. However, a user testing session (‘observational research) might only the exact point where a user struggles, but fails to explain the underlying root cause. 
This is where a user interview (‘self reported method) can be quite useful in ie tidying the root cause of what the user was thinking at the moment they experienced the struggle during the aforementioned testing session. 


What are the benefits and limitations of remote vs. in-person UX research? 

There is value in both remote and in person UX research. Remote UX research allows a UX designer to conduct research in a much faster manner by accessing users in different geographical locations. 
This is particularly useful because it allows the UX designers to understand how users in different geographical locations interact with their product, behave, think, have differential needs and pain points. 
But, it makes it more difficult for a designer to establish rapport amnongst the users in a remote environment. 
This is particularly the case for remote user tests where the designer is unable to visibly see the user because their camera is turned off or their internet is slow. 
This can lead to the designer missing vital contextual information, such as cues and body language. Remote testing makes it more difficult to establish rapport as well since users have less time available in contrast to in person testing where facilities are shared by the user and the designers. This gives them a better opportunity to establish rapport, and this in turn can lead to the user feeling more relaxed. This is particularly true for user interviews.
In contrast to remote research, in person research allows a designer to be in the same physical location as the users. 
This in turn leads to the designer being able to observe the users in a manner that allows them to observe subtle cues. 
It becomes more easier in person to identify frustration, annoyance, joy, and gain insight into the mental model of a user. 
However, in person methods can be far more labour intensive (emotionally),time consuming and expensive.  


What is the jobs to be done framework? What are the different types of user jobs? 

The jobs to be done can be conceptualised as a unique framework that helps UX designers understand what exactly a user is trying to accomplish when they are using a particular product. 
The jobs to be done framework does not focus on the demographic aspects of the user (‘who they are, and what they do).
Instead the focus is entirely on what outcome are they trying to achieve and what problem they are attempting to solve. The underlying premise of the jobs to be done framework is that users tend to use a product because they are attempting to solve a problem or make some kind of progress in their life. 
Under a Jobs to be done framework, user jobs can be categorised into three key categories. 
A functional job refers to the task a user is attempting to complete and they have a specific outcome in mind. For example, a focus bear user might want to complete their uni assessments in a timely manner. 
In contrast, an emotional job is the users attempt to relive themselves from the emotional consequences of failing to complete a specific task. 
he users underlying aim is not simply to complete the assessment, but it is to have freedom from the guilt of wasting another day procrastinating on their university assessment. On the contrary, a social job is about the users attempt to signal at a social level their identify, social status, sense of belonging, and how they would like to be perceived. 
An example of this would be a user thinking they want it complete their uni assessments on time because they do not wish to be a slacker or someone that is not hard working.

What challenges might arise when collecting self-reported user feedback? 

Self reported feedback has certain limitations. Many users fail to properly articulate their behaviour because they themselves lack insight into their own behaviour. At other times users may fail to notice certain issues and still say “no it was fine”. 
This issue is quite common and can easily be picked up by usability tests. This is because we can observe users experiencing issues, becoming confused, hesitating and second guessing themselves as they try to navigate through the user testing scenarios by interacting with the products user interface. 
Furthermore, another issue with self reported feedback from users is that many people that think they can better help by providing solutions instead of articulating the problem or underlying issues. 
For example, a focus bear user asked to do testing for the purpose of improving onboarding  might say “I don’t understand why you added a skip option” without really explaining further. 
Self reported feedback from users can also be more susceptible to biases since they can forget crucial details and want to appear helpful by giving some type of response, despite not knowing if they are actually helping with their feedback. 


How can UX designers avoid bias when conducting research? 

UX designers can best avoid biases during research by ensuring they properly document evidence. Furthermore, it is important that they document the evidence in a manner by ensuring that the assumptions and preferences they have as a designer do not override their own judgement. Asking leading questions during interviews is one example of causing inadvertent bias during research. If a designer that is leading a user interview session asks a user “‘did you find the onboarding process hard or confusing”, it can cause the user to respond in a manner, even inadvertently, to please the interviewer. Bias is most common when UX designers are looking for evidence to support their already beliefs independent of objective reality. If a designer is only seeking confirmation that their redesign of the focus bear onboarding process is superior to the existing design, then they become more susceptible to noticing positive comments regarding this matter.  They do not look as closely to issues or problems with the onboarding process that users might be having during a user testing session. Another way to minimise the impact of bias is by observing users in addition to conducting surveys and user interviews. It helps designers find things that a survey or user interview fail in highlighting.
