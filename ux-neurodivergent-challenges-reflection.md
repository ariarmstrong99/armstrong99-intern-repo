What are common methods for analyzing usability test results? 

Analysing usability testing results often tend to focus on identifying certain patterns in user behaviour instead of relying on the individual statements or feedback provided by the users. 
The ultimate goal is to gain an understanding of where exactly users seem to struggle, why those particular struggles tend to exist and how often those struggles appear across different participants.
A common approach to analysing test results is thematic analysis. Once a usability test recording is evaluated, similar overservarions are grouped into themes. 
For example, if focus bear users found the settings menu to be confusing, misinterpreted icons, and struggled to navigate this menu. 
This issue can be listed under the category of “‘navigation and discoverability issues”.


another approach that is used is called affinity mapping. This is where people often write down observations, quotes, behaviours, and pain points into individual notes and then proceed to categorise them into similar clusters. Conducting an affinity map makes it easier for researchers to identify patterns that have not been obvious when individual sessions are reviewed. Affinity maps are useful when there is a large amount of data to go through from user interviews and usability tests.

In contrast, a task based analysis is whereby each testing scenario is analysed based on a predetermined success metric. 
For example, if users are asked to create a blocking schedule via the settings screen, researchers can measure the success rate of this task by seeing how many users managed to complete the task successfully, how long it took them, where exactly they became confused and if help was required. 
Issues can also be evaluated on severity ratings. This is where researcher will analyse each issue based on frequency of occurence, impact and how persistent the issue tends to be. 
An issue that impacts a large number of participants and stops users from completing tasks would be classed as being high in severity , whereas an issue due to a minor aesthetic issue would be classed as low severity and impact.
For usability analysis to be effective, it is imperative to also use user quotes to back up findings. Designers need to focus on what users are actually doing then on they should be doing. 
If a participant states that “the onboarding is fine” but then hesitates repeatedly throughout the process, takes a long amount of time to complete the process and are visibly frustrated as they try to complete the onboaring, then this behaviour needs to be probed further instead of their verbal statement. 
Quotes can help because they can help explain the thought process of a user and reveal their mental models, however they need to be interpreted alongside behaviour that is observed. 


How can UX designers avoid bias when interpreting feedback? 

One of the key issues in UX research is being able to successfully separate what a user does from what researchers actually see. Bias takes place when a designer becomes emotionally attached to a particular design solution and then tried to find evidence to justify this solutioning. 
This can be avoided by approaching user testing with an open mind and being curious instead of trying to prove that their design is accurate. 
Users may say that they like a particular feature, but if they are struggling to use it, become confused by it, or require assistance then the behaviour needs to probed further because it signals underlying issues with the design and usability issues. 
When users give feedback, designers should not place too much emphasis on one individual participant. Sometimes an individual stands out in the mind of a designer due to their personality traits and their ability to articulate themselves. 
This can lead to the designer placing more emphasis on their input instead of on other designers. Designers need to see if other users are dealing with the same issues.
Bias can be minimise by adhering to a objective way of writing. Designers can state “7 of out 8 users failed to finish onboarding successfully” instead of “onboarding is confusing and users hated it”. 


What’s the difference between actionable insights vs. subjective opinions? 

Actionable insights are often supported by tangible evidence and can lead to concrete design decisions. 
In contrast, subjective opinions are simply personal preferences that do not represent a user need and not necessarily based on evidence. 
Users will invariably share their opinios during user testing sessions. Their statements can be quite helpful , but they do not mean that their comments indicate a usability problem. A user test participant might say “‘I don’t like the dark mode interface, I hate dark colours”. 
This is a personal preference and does not indicate issues with the interface. An actionable insight in contrast allows a designer to identify a pattern that will reveal a user problem when probed further. 
If multiple participants are struggling to navigate to the next screen durijg onboarding because they find the “next” button, and become frustrated when they have to look around for the button, then the action insight is that not enough hierarchy is given to “next” button and it lacks visibility and/ or is not positioned properly. 
This is a genuine insight that can lead to improvements in the interface. 
Subjective opinions simply are what a user might like or dislike.  
Competent UX designers do not make design decision on sugjective opinions. They look for evidence and Patterns in the research and in testing sessions. 


How do you prioritize user feedback when multiple users have conflicting requests? 

Conflicting feedback tends to take place because each user has different mental models and goals. One user might emphasise customisation options more since they are adept technologically. In contrast, another user might ask for a simpler interface because they get overwhelmed by customisation options since they are not adept at using technology. It is not possible to implement both versions of the feedback. A competent UX designer will investigate why the feedback was provided in the first place. Users might simply be asking for a product that feels like it can be managed properly by them and that it can be used efficiently.
A useful approach is to ensure that feedback is prioritised based on how it impacts user goals and business goals. A lot of the issues stop users from completing important tasks and can affect business metrics negatively. These should take precedence over any requests made by the user directly. 
If multiple users comment on their dislike for dark mode and dark colours, it is not an important issue unless they are vision impaired. Designers should always try to establish how often an issue takes place how impactful the issue is on user needs and business goals. 
It is also important to segment users because when users give out conflicting feedback, it may be because they are entirely different population groups entirely. 
Designers need to find solutions that can accommodate multiple demographics.

If one user complains about a feature but others like it, how should UX handle it? 

If a user complains about a particular feature while other users like that particular feature, a competent designer needs to avoid taking biased view and avoid extremes. 
They need to properly investigate if the compliant does actually high,GitHub underlying usability issues or if this is just opinion/preference. 
It is important for a UX designer to understand what the user is struggling with exactly. A complaint does not mean that the feature is broken. 
The problem could be that the onboarding process takes too long, is confusing, has too many steps with lots of overwhelming information and access to grant permissions at a lot of levels without adequately gaining user trust or conveying value proposition to the users. 
A designers job will be to identify what the underlying issue is instead of simply reacting to criticism. Designers also need to analyse the evidence. 
If a lot of the users are facing the same problem , then this highlights an issue that needs to be probed further. But if a single user highlights an issue that can Impact users then it needs to be looked at as well. 
The designer needs to look at evidence. At times when a designer looks at user interview recordings, user feedback from reviews, user testing reports and analytics, a single complaint can lead to the designer discovering issues with accessibility if they look for patterns and go through the research properly. 


How can patterns in feedback help identify real UX issues? 

Patterns in feedback allow a designer to differentiate between subjective opinions and issues with the design. When a user test is conducted, designers should be placing emphasis on observing user behaviour instead of focusing on individual comments. 
Finding patterns and focusing on patterns is helpful because users tend to articulate issues in differential ways. Patterns allow the designer to identify recurring themes that then allow designers to uncover the root cause of an issue rather than focusing on individual comments from users. 
UX researchers place a lot of emphasis on research synthesis and thematic analysis for this particular reason. 
5 different users might articulate the same issue in different ways, but analysing patterns helps the designer uncover the root cause of the issue by focusing on patterns in user behaviour instead of simply focusing on their verbal feedback. 


What are the risks of making changes based on limited or unverified feedback? 

The risk of making changes based on feedback that is unverified can lead to design teams solving the wrong issue or leading to the introduction of new usability issues that do not exist. 
UX decisions are always grounded in evidence instead of being made from listening to random comments. There is a massive risk of overreacting to feedback. When a designer makes design decisions based on unverified feedback it can lead to issues with feature creep. 
By responding more and more to requests at an individual level, a product can start to gradually become far more cluttered with extra settings, options  and functionality. Whilst each change can seem rational at an individual level, when these changes add up they can lead to the product being more hard to use. 
Also, when frequent changes are made to feedback that is not verified, it can lead to an inconsistent user experience. This is because responding reactively to individual feedback will fragment the interface and deviate from the overall design strategy. 
Futhermore, making design decisions based on unverified feedback, even if it comes users, can lead to wastage of company resources. Each design decision takes time to Implement and test. If changes are made that are based on unverified feedback, then the team might spend a significant amount of time trying to build a product that does not lead to improvements in user experience. 
A competent UX designer always seeks to validate any feedback by looking at research, user interviews, user testing, and analytics to try and find the underlying issues instead of just implementing solutions.
