
What common UX barriers affect neurodivergent users? 

Neurodivergent users often face various barriers. This is particularly the case of individuals diagnosed with ADHD and autism. They face issues with filtering out distractions, processing information, remembering information, and struggling to navigate the interface due to being overwhelmed cognitively. 
Neurodivergent users, particularly individuals with adhd, often get overwhelmed easily when an interface has too many options, feels too cluttered, has too many visual effects or is visually heavy. A lot of users will then struggle to focus because they will feel unsure of where to divert their attentional resources. 
This can often lead to difficult making a decision, abandonment of task and general frustration. Individuals with adhd are often susceptible since their working memory is limited and becomes easily overwhelmed when they are forced into processing large amounts of imformation. 
Another issue is that of poor information hierarchy. If the important actions a user needs to take are not going to stand out properly, a lot of users will then struggle to identify what needs to be done next. 
A lot of neurodivergent users tend to benefit when the interface is able to properly communicate important actions and provide them with guidance on where their attention needs to be directed on the interface.
When the interface is quite distracting in nature, it can present challenges for people with adhd. When these individuals are presented with notifications, pop ups and animations, it can destroy their focus and then make it harder for them to re-engage with the task again. 
Task switching depletes people with adhd a lot. 
On the other hand, initiating tasks and issues with executive dysfunction are quite common for people with adhd. 
They understand that they need to do certain tasks, but they struggle with intertia to get the process started.when an interface forced these people to make multiple decisions, navigate across multiple screens or are forced to take more steps, all of this reduces the rate of task completion for these people with adhd. 
In contrast, autistic individuals often struggle when there are sudden changes to the interface, and when navigation patterns  are inconsistent. These individuals tend to benefit from a user experience that is predictable and where actions tend to produce clear and consistent results.
Time blindness is another particular issue that is frequently experience by people with adhd. A lot of users might struggle with estimating how long it might take to complete a certain task, they may also lose track of time due to hyper focus on a particular activity or task and also fail to properly gauge the level of effort that they might need to invest in completing a task. 
Any interface that tends to provide clear progress indicators, and representations of time in a visual manner can be quite helpful to these people.
It is also important to make sure that language used throughout the app is clear, consistent and unambiguous. Furthermore, when feedback is given, it needs to be clear, otherwise it will create challenges.
When buttons, instructions or labels are vague, it can lead to users being unsure if they have completed a task properly or what particular action they need to take next. 

How can predictability, clear feedback, and reduced distractions improve usability? 

Predictability will improve the overall usability by helping users understand how an interface behaves. When the interactions, buttons, animations, navigation, and language across the app is consistent and predictable, that means that users are not constantly forced to learn new behaviour. 
This is quite helpful for users with autism since these users tend to prefer structure and consistency. Similarly, individuals with adhd tend to become quite overwhelmed when an interface behaves in an unexpected manner.
When clear feedback is provided to users, it will enable them to understand the result of the actions that they take. Each time that a user interacts with a system, they receive feedback abou the action that has taken place. 
Without appropriate feedback, users will repeat the same actions or simply think the system is broken. Clear feedback can also reduce uncertainty for users that are neurodivergent and lead to reduction in cognitive overwhelm since they understand immediately if they have executed a task or action properly. 
On the other hand, having reduced distractions is also important for users in order to help them maintain their focus on their primary goal. A lot of digital products tend to bombard the users with notifications, flashy animations, and calls to action. For a person with adhd, these type of distractions can lead to task switching and deplete their ability to focus on task. 
It is necessary to remove any unnecessary distractions and seek to present information to such users in a manner that will not lead to cognitive overwhelm. 


Why do overly complex interfaces cause issues for people with ADHD? 

People with adhd tend to become overwhelmed when an interface is quite complex in nature. Furthermore, these individuals also struggle to process information that is complex or when they are present with a significant amount of complex information. 
This is because they have issues with working memory and their information processing tends to be slower than neurotypical individuals. This means that these aforementioned individuals have to expend more cognitive resources in order to make decisions and stay on task when an interface is complex.  
Cognitive overwhelm is quite common for people with adhd. This usually occurs when an interface is too cluttered. This can lead to depletion of cognitive bandwidth for such users and it will then cause them to become frustrated, slow their decision making ability and can trigger avoidance behaviours. 
This is because a complex interface combined with cognitive overwhelm can exacerbate executive dysfunction for people with adhd. A lot of individuals with adhd already struggle a lot with planning, initiating tasks, and being able to follow through on a multi step process. 
When presented with too many choices, they shut down. This in turn also triggers indecision due to slower ability to make decisions when overwhelmed.  



What are some real-world design examples that improve accessibility for neurodivergent users? 

Design that is neurodivergent friendly often tends to have a specific characteristic. They reduce the overall cognitive effort a user needs to expend, they make the interface more predictable, and they allow users to stay on task. An example of this is tiimo. This app helps people with adhd by helping them plan better. The app uses schedules that are represented in visual terms, timelines, and uses a task sequencing that is clear in nature. Users do not need to hold Information in their working memory and because time is represented visually, users can deal with time blindness and task initiation much better.

Duolingo is another example because it has several examples of neurodivergent friendly design practices. Lessons are often broken down into smaller chunks and any progress that occurs is clearly visible. 
The feedback provided is also immediate in nature and the users will always know what particular action they need to take next. The interface effectively reduces ambiguity and manages to create a highly consistent user experience that can be quite helpful to users that struggle with attention issues or cognitive overwhelm.

How can Focus Bear’s design choices make tasks easier for neurodivergent users?

Focus bear can make design decisions that make things easier for neurodivergent users by reducing the mental work that often stops a neurodivergent user from taking any action. 
For example, a guided routine that makes tasks much easier because users will not have to remember each and every step. The app will basically show one step at a time, this in turn reduces the cognitive overwhelm experienced by a user and will help them keep moving. 
By ensuring that the overall user experience is consistent, and predictable, focus bear will let users complete tasks instead of having that energy be wasted on deciding what needs to be done next. 



If a feature is technically useful but cognitively overwhelming, how should it be redesigned? 

If a feature is useful, but tends to overwhelm users cognitively, then it needs to be redesigned in a manner that ensures users will have access to the benefits without actually being exposed to any complexity that is unnecessary. 
Redesigning the feature based on the principle of progressive disclosure could be helpful. Under such an approach, the advanced functionality of the feature would be hidden until it becomes relevant for the user. 
This would allow users to focus their attention on completing tasks without actually being overwhelmed by any options that they do not actually need. Once users become more experienced, any additional functionality that exists can be revealed progressively to prevent overwhelming the users and ensuring feature still remains useful.  
It is also helpful to break down a complex process or feature into smaller processes or steps. Instead of presenting a lot of choices and actions for the user to take, it is better to guide them through one action at a time. This approach can be quite helpful for neurodivergent users that experience indecision and cognitive overwhelm.


What are some small UX adjustments that can significantly improve user experience for neurodivergent users? 

The user experience of neurodivergent users can be significantly improved by ensuring that they are not overwhelmed cognitively, have more clarity on how the interfaces behaves, and their ability to stay focused is not impaired by bombardign them with flashy animations, notifications, unncesssary nudges and calls to actions that are visually heavy. 
Making improvements to the visual hierarchy in the interface can be quite helpful since the primary actions become far more prominent and this in turn can reduce the competition from any secondary actions that helps a user determine what particular action needs to be taken next. Using labels that are clear and have proper descriptions with unambiguous language is also helpful. 
Users need to understand what a specific  button, setting or feature actually does. Making good usage of progress indicators is also quite helpful. Especially during onboarding, completion of routines or during multi step workflows. Showing to the users exactly where they are and how much progress is still remaining managed to reduce their anxiety and keeps their momentum ongoing. 
Having consistency is also quite important because keeping aspects of the interface uniform across the app makes users less confused. Also, it reducing the number of decisions, and  actions a user needs to make leads to less cognitive overwhelm,.
