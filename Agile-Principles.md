What is agile and how does it differ from traditional project management?

Traditional project management is also known as the waterfall approach. This essentially entails a process that is linear in nature. Each phase of the project is divided into linear and sequential manner. 
This type of approach tends to work well when requirements are clear and concrete. However, it is a lot harder to make changes to the project and also be a lot more difficult to implement. 
In contrast, agile method is more flexible and interative in nature. In a project operating from the agile approach, all work is completed on an incremental basis in cycles or sprints. 
Refinements are made on a continual basis based on feedback. Instead of working on having concrete requirements, a team operating from the agile approach delivers working solutions early on and also gathers inputs from various stakeholders and users. 
When goals change, team members adapt quickly. 

What are the core values and principles of agile?

There are 4 key values that are outlined in the agile manifesto:

People and the interactions they have take priority over business processes and tools used
Working software should be prioritised over comprehensive documentation
Collaborating with customers should take precedence over contract negotiations 
Being reflexive and being adaptive instead of sticking to a rigid plan’

These  values all establish that there is a greater level of value to be gained by focusing on human collaboration, outcomes that are functional in nature, a deeper level of customer involvememt and being adaptable in nature instead of being rigid.


What are the differences between scrum and kanban, and when is each used?

The agile paradigm has two frameworks that are often utilised. One of them is called scrum and the second is know as kanban. Scrum uses sprints, with each sprint typically lasting 2-4 s weeks. 
The team commits to a set of objectives and tasks for each sprint prior to its commencement.  All progress is then reviewed via daily standup meetings, sprint reviews and holding retrospective meetings. 
Scrum is quite popular when a team is attempting to develop a new feature or a product.
In contrast, kanban does not have any defined sprints and instead relies on a continuous approach whereby tasks are moved across a visual board as the capacity to undertake opens up. All tasks are categorised into three groupings, to be done, in progress and completed.  
Since there are no defined sprints, any work that needs to be conducted is undertaken based on priority and this process occurs on an ongoing basis. Kanban is often used when priorities and goals shift quite quickly. 
It is also useful in settings whereby teams can manage ongoing operational or support work.

What do you think are the biggest benefits and challenges of agile?


The agile paradigm encourages teams to learn faster and adapt continually during their work process. When teams work from an agile paradigm they often deliver work on an incremental basis that allows feedback to be delivered more often. 
It also allows teams to identify problems early on and respond to the changing requirements also reduce the overall risk of building features that simply do not meet the needs and goals of users. 
The agile paradigm also encourages a much highe level of collaboration between designers, developers, project managers, and product managers. This facilitates improved communication between the aforementioned parties and can lead to better design decisions.
Despite this, there are still challenges with the agile paradigm. Since it requires a strong level of communication. 
If goals remain unclear or if priorities shift too often, then teams can end up becoming more confused  and engage in context switching on a more regular basis. This can lead to drift. 
Taking an agile approach is also a lot more uncomfortable for people who like things to be preplanned in advance and have more certainty. 
Agile requires tolerating a degree of uncertainty and also being able to refine solutions on an iterative basis instead of defining everything in advance.

Summarise the main differences between scrum and kanban in your own words?

The primary difference between scrum and kanban is in the structure. Scrum uses a different structure and relies on grouping work into sprints. Each sprint has a unique set of goals, expected outcomes and area of focus for work.  
There are also stand up meetings, review meetings, sprint planning sessions and retrospective meetings to manage sprints. All progress during a sprint is evaluated when a sprint culminates. 
In contrast, kanban is a lot more flexible in nature. All work is conducted and managed on a continual basis. Tasks are conducted based on priority and capacity. 
Tasks can be reallocated based on shifting priorities with ease and this type of framework is most suitable in an environment that has constant changes occurring and tends to be more fast paced.
