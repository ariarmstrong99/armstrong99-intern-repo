Developers: in a tesm operating from an agile framework, developers tend to build and maintain the product by writing code, implementing features and then fixing bugs 
Quality assurance: a team member that is assigned the role of QA tests a particular product in order to identify the defects in a product and then verify whether those features work as intended.
Product manager: the role of a product manager is quite broad, but in a team operating from an agile framework, product managers typically tend to define the priorities and get clarity on this, and then make sure that the product team is focused on delivering value to the business  and users.
Agile project manager: they are responsible for facilitating the workflow, removing any blocks that do exist and help the team follow agile practices more effictively.
UX designers and researchers: they attempt to understand business needs and user needs . They also design the UI, and test the designs to ensure that any solution that is created provides users with a good user experience. 
They also ensure that the right problem is being solved in the first place.
Stakeholders: in an agile team, they provide business with more context, strategic direction and give feedback on priories.
Customer support: they share insights from users, they are also responsible identifying any problems that occur on a regular basis. 
They then help the wider team gain an understanding of the real world issues based on their interactions with customers. 


How do these roles collaborate in kanban vs scrum?

In a team operating within the frame of scrum, any collaboration that does occur follow a proper structure. This is called a sprint. 
The product manager here ensures that any work that needs to be done is properly reviewed. He also decides what work is important based on the user needs, business goals and other priorities. 
While planning a sprint, a product manager will give the team work with the highest level of priority. The team then chooses their work tasks based on what they can realistically finish during the upcoming sprint. 
For example,a 2 week sprint may have 2 rounds of user testing listed, but the product manager can then determine whether this is feasible or not based on the information available. 
They might then decide to focus on improving another area of the app or product if they determine 2 rounds of user testing may not possible within the timeframe. 
In contrast, developers and QA people in scrum often commit to a set of tasks for each sprint. 
The scrum master or agile project manager helps the team remove any blockages to ensure everything is efficient and smooth for the rest of the team. UX designers are responsible for contributing to product research and user research, design, and then help other team members by briefing them with feedback on design decisions. 
This tends to occur during the regular meetings such as standup meetings etc. 
On the contrary, in a team operating from a kanban framework, the nature of collaboration is far more fluid. All work can be visualised on a shared board, and team members are able to focus on a particular task based on their ability to execute and. Priority of the task. 
An agile project manager tends to focus on the actual work and whether there are any issues that prevent the work from getting done. They do not focus on facilitating meetings and agile ceremonies. 
UX designers under a kanban framework tend to add designs and specifications to issues, whilst developers focus on the task with the highest amount of priority. The QA then tests each issue when it is ready. 
The difference is in the nature of collaboration. In scrum, roles are structured through sprints. In kanban, team members can collaborate more freely and tends to occur through a steady stream of work.
