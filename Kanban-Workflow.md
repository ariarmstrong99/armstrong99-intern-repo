
How does a kanban board work, and how does it help manage workflow?

A kanban board works by visually representing work tasks as they move through different stages of the development process. 
All tasks are depicted as cards or tickets and categorised based on their status into  “to do, in progress, needs review, done, backlog, blocked”. 
All team members are able to pick a task when they are able to focus on doing a particular task and do not have to wait for sprints. 
This makes it much easier for a team to track their work process and identify specific barriers that may be slowing down their overall workflow. 
For example, if a series of tasks begin to pile up under the “to do” category, it can be an indication that the team is struggling due to various reasons. 
This could lead to further Investigation in order to pinpoint the root cause and alleviate the problem so that the workflow can resume at a steady pace. Such as staff shortages or struggling team members. 


What do the different columns on a kanban board represent?

Each column on a kanban board visually depicts the stage or phase of each task as it currently stands. 
For example, all tasks in the “‘backlog” category are identified as tasks that have yet to be started. 
Similarly, all tasks categorised as “done” are tasks that have been completed successfully in accordance to the requirements of the organisation. Any task that has been categorised as “In progress” means that those tasks are currently being worked on by the team. 
Tasks that have been categorised as “to do/ready” are tasks that have been  marked as tasks that need to be done and are ready to be worked on in their currrent state. 
Tasks that have been categorised as “‘review/testing” are ones that are currently being reviewed by the team or tested. Any task that has been categorised as “blocked” is a task that cannot be progressed on further due to some type of ongoing issue, such as technical constraints that are preventing the work from being done.


How do tasks move through the board, and who is responsible for updating them?

The way a task progresses on a kanban board is fairly simple, they tend to begin in the backlog area and then they progress onto the  “to do” category when they are ready to be worked on. 
Once work commenced on the tasks that are marked as “to do”, they progress onto the “in progress” category immediately after somebody from the team starts to work on those specific tasks. 
Once the tasks from the “in progress” category are completed, they then progress onto the “review/testing” area where they are evaluated. If they are meeting the criteria for completion, then they are categorised as “done”. 
When a team member works on a particular task, they are the ones that need to ensure that its status is updated properly. 
For example, if I as a UX intern start to work on a usability testing task, then I would move it to the “in progress” category from the “to do” category when I start the task. 
Once I complete the task, I would move it to the “review/testing” category. 
The UX supervisor responsible for overseeing my work will then proceed to review my work and then move it to the “done” category if it adequately meets the completion criteria. 
It is very important to accurately update tasks under teh kanban system as it allows the entire team to visualise how work tasks are progressing. 
In this manner, the team can identify any issues that come up and alleviate those issues before they compound into more severe problems.


What are the benefits of limiting work in progress?

When tasks categorised as “in progress” are restricted to a set number, it forced the team to focus on a few tasks and prioritise their completion prior to starting new tasks without properly completing ones that have already been commenced. 
For neurodivergent individuals, novelty plays a huge factor in being able to stay on task. Novelty might lead to neurodivergent individuals, particularly ones with adhd working on multiple tasks simultaneously and switching between them without adequately finishing the current ones they have already begun. 
It can lead to them forgetting about the tasks they already started if they enter a state of hyper focus on a new task that they started simultaneously. It is much more beneficial if the focus is on a few key tasks that have already been started, with the aim to complete them properly at a high standard. 
This not only minimises context switching, which can lead to mental fatigue, but also ensures high quality output from team members. 

How does kanban help manage priorities and avoid overload?

Kanban allows team members to visualise all work tasks based on their current status. 
Such as “backlog, to be done, In progress, review/testing, done, blocked”. Tasks that have the higher level of priority will sit at the very top of the backlog queue and once they are already to be started, into the “to do” category. 
This allows team members to identify tasks of the highest level of importance and focus their attention to those tasks. This system also encourages a high level of accountability as team members can see what task is being worked on by what specific team member. 
It ensures that too many tasks are not started simultaneously due to limits placed on “in progress” tasks. The kanban system allows team members to identify clearly how many tasks a specific team is already working on at present. 
This prevents those team members from becoming more overloaded with tasks if they already have a series of tasks assigned to them and those tasks are still within the “‘in progress” category. 
The team can simply reallocate a task to another team member in such an instance instead of allowing a pile up of tasks to occur for a specific team member, which could overwhelm further and slow them down significantly. 
This in turn would cause more problems over time . 



How can you improve your workflow using kanban principles?

As a neurodivergent individual with adhd, I can leverage the principles of kanban to my advantage in order optimise the quality of my work related outputs. Context switching is an issue that can slow me down considerably and lead to mental fatigue. 
By focusing on one task at a time instead of commencing several tasks at the same time, I reduce cognitive overwhelm and focus entirely on finishing the current task I have already started. Under a kanban system, there would be a limit to have many tasks I can have under the “in progress” category. 
This would keep me accountable to complete the task(s) I have already started. I would only focus on doing one task with my full attention as a neurodivergent individual to ensure I can deliver the best quality possible. 
In a kanban system, I would also be able to see what tasks other team members are working on at present. This would allow me to identify what task I would need to focus on after I complete the current task I have already commenced. 
This ensures that the team delivers work outputs in a streamlined manner. Kanban also allows other team members to see how long I have spent on a task categorised as “in progress”. 
It can help team members spot immediately if I am struggling with a task if I have taken an inordinate amount of time working  on a particular task. 
I can then receive any assistance if needed to complete the tasks.This would make it much easier to identify problems, even if one person fails to identify an issue since all work tasks are visualised under the kanban system based on specific categories.
