What are key UX metrics used to measure usability and engagement? 

Key UX metrics can be conceptualised essentially as measurements that help designers understand whether users are able to successfully use a product. In addition, these aforementioned metrics also help designers understand whether or not the user experience is indeed managing to achieve the intended outcomes.

Some of the most common key metrics are:

Task success rate: this refers to the percentage of users that manage to successfully complete a task within a product. For example, how many users are able to start their focus timer on the focus bear app without having to look elsewhere or become confused.
Time on task: this refers to how long a user will take to complete a particular task within the product. If users tend to spend more time on a particular task, it could be an indication of an underlying problem.
Error rate: this metric refers to how often a user makes a mistake while completing a particular task, such as navigating to the wrong screen or simply not knowing how to proceed to the next step.
Drop off rate: this UX metric refers to the percentage of users that actually abandon a process within the product without managing to complete it. An example of this could be users becoming frustrated with how long the onboarding is and then deciding to abandon the process altogether.
User satisfaction: this UX metric refers to the feedback that is collected via surveys or via usability testing in regards to how pleasurable the user experience was for users using the product.
Retention rate: this specific UX metric refers to the percentage of users that continue to use the product over a specific period of time.
Engagement metrics: this particular UX metric refers to aspects such as product usage, the frequency of a session, and how users are able to return to the product. 



What is the difference between quantitative (e.g., task completion rate) and qualitative (e.g., user sentiment) metrics?

Quantitative metrics often tell you what is happening. In contrast, qualitiative metrics help you explain why it is occurring. Quantitative metrics are often numerical in nature. They tend to show us how users behave and this is often tracked over a period of time. An example of this would be things like the onboarding completion rate for focus bear app and the drop off rate during this process. 
On the other hand, qualitative metrics are often focused more on user metrics such as opinions, mental models, and experiences. 
They essentially help designers understand how a user will feel about the particular product and why they tend to behave in that manner. 
Usability test results, and user satisfaction surveys are an example of qualitative metrics.
In the context of onboarding for focus bear app, quantitative data might show that 20% of users abandon the onboarding process altogether. 
In contrast, qualitative research data will help identify why users tend to abandon the onboarding process. 
Is it due to confusion, is the process too long or do they feel the app does not provide enough value.


How do tools like heatmaps, session recordings, and analytics dashboards help track UX performance? 

Ux designers can rely on various tools in order to understand how a user tends to interact with the product interface when the product is already live.  
For example, a heatmap can show where exactly users are clicking, tapping, or focusing their attend on the screen. This allows UX designers  to properly identify if key actions a user needs to perform are occurring and being noticed. 
It also shows a UX designer where exactly users are focusing and it highlights how a user may focus on a wrong area, if it occurs. 
Similarly, recordings of user testing sessions and user interviews can successfully reveal where exactly users have issues, become confused, click on an element  or abandon the task altogether. 
This essentially allows the UX designer successfully pin point key usability issues with more ease that metrics may sometimes fail to identify. 
Furthermore, analytics dashboards can help UX designers with giving them access to quantitative data about things like user behaviour. 
Within the context of focus bear app onboarding, it can highlight important imformation such as the onboarding completion rate, rate of drop off , and task completion rate. 
Analytics dashboards essentially allow UX designers to determine if a user is completing important actions. 


How can designers use A/B testing data to validate improvements? 

A/b testing In UX helps a designer determine if a design change will lead to improvements in the overall user behaviour. 
During an a/b test, a group of users is presented with one version of the design (version a: this is the current state design), whereas another group of users  is presented with a second version of the design (version b: this corresponds to the future state or redesigned version). 
The design team then proceeds to compare the key metrics they have from the tests for the two aforementioned versions. Within the context of redesigning the onboarding experience for mobile version of focus bear, the a/b test would seek to get imformation on the onboarding completion rate, drop off rate, the total time taken to complete the setup and user retention after the first week for users. 
During the a/b test if users that are seeing the version b tend to be more prone to abandoning the onboarding process more frequently than those users seeing version a, it becomes very palpable that the redesign has made things worse. 
A/b testing is useful because it lets designers evaluate their design and design decisions based on actual evidence. 



If a UX metric shows poor user engagement, how would you determine the cause? 

When a UX ends up showing a poor level of user engagement, it is important to properly investigate the root cause via quantitative and qualitative research. 
For example, within the context of the focus bear onboarding process on mobile, if it is found out via analytics that users are abandoning the onboarding process altogether, the first step would be to identify where exactly the drop off is occurring. 
This can be determined  occur by reviewing the recordings of user testing sessions in order to properly observe how users are interacting with the onboarding process. 
I would also conduct user interviews with some of the users that have abandoned the onboarding in order to get more insight into why users were abandoning the onboarding process.


What are the risks of relying too much on numbers instead of qualitative feedback? 

The issue with relying too heavily on quantitative data as opposed to qualitative data is that you can get insight into what users are doing, but you fail to understand properly why exactly they are doing it in that specific manner. 
For example, within the context of the focus bear onboarding process on mobile, it may indicate that only 20% of users are completing the onboarding process. 
This particular metric tells us that there is a problem but it fails to adequately explain why it occurs and it certainly fails to explain the root cause of the problem. 
When qualitative research is not conducted, the product team can not have a proper understanding about the root cause. Another significant risk of relying too heavily on quantitative research data is that teams are able to optimise for metrics while also failing to address the acutal issues prevalent within the existing designs. 
For example, within the context of tje onboarding for focus bear app on mobile, the process could be optimised so that users complete the onboarding process. 
However, user interviews with users might reveal that they “‘just wanted to get the process done” and “did not fully understand what was covered in the process and now have no clue what do now with the app”.


How can UX designers use metrics to convince stakeholders to invest in usability improvements? 

UX designers need to convince key stakeholders, such as the product manager, in order to have them address usability issues that have been identified. The best way to convince the key stakeholders is by demonstrating to them that the usability issues have an adverse impact on user experience of the product and also lead to business outcomes not being met.  
UX metrics can be incredibly useful in helping the UX designer convince the key stakeholders to make usability improvements to the designs. 
This is because UX metrics can help a UX designer show that “only 20% of people are completing the onboarding for focus bear app on mobile. 
A significant portion of users are abandoning the onboarding process altogether and based on user test results, they get very agitated, annoyed and fail to understand the value proposition for the focus bear app. 
They have not Been fully convinced by the onboarding process that it is worth investing their time”.  By clearly demonstrating that the usability issues impact  outcomes like rate of onboaring completion, and user retention, that ultimately hinder the ability of the product to meet business goals, is the best way in which a UX designer can leverage UX metrics in order to get key stakeholders such as the product manager to action important usability improvements to the product.
